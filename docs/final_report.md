## Abstract

The U.S. generator interconnection process has become a major bottleneck for clean-energy deployment. Thousands of gigawatts of proposed generation are waiting in interconnection queues, while most projects are eventually withdrawn after developers have already incurred meaningful pre-development costs. This project builds a machine-learning pipeline for early-stage queue intelligence: predicting project withdrawal, network-upgrade cost bucket, and study timeline from publicly available queue features. The core methodological contribution is an adaptation of physics-informed neural networks from Raissi, Perdikaris, and Karniadakis (2019). Instead of enforcing a PDE residual, the neural network cost classifier is trained with a monotonicity residual that penalizes predictions where estimated upgrade cost decreases as requested MW capacity increases, all else held equal. The exact quantitative results are generated by the pipeline and stored in `outputs/metrics.json` so the final written claims stay tied to the latest reproducible run.

## 1. Introduction

The U.S. power grid is facing a growing interconnection queue crisis. Renewable, storage, hybrid, and conventional generation projects often wait years before receiving final interconnection study results. During that time, developers must make siting, financing, and permitting decisions without reliable early estimates of whether the project will survive the queue, how expensive network upgrades may be, or how long the study process will take.

The project goal is to predict three outcomes before or near the queue-filing stage:

- Whether a project is likely to be withdrawn.
- The likely network-upgrade cost bucket: $0-1M, $1-5M, $5-20M, or $20M+.
- The expected study timeline in months.

This framing is important for civil and infrastructure engineering because interconnection delays directly affect grid expansion, resource adequacy, decarbonization timelines, and capital allocation. A model that identifies high-risk projects earlier could support better site screening and reduce wasted engineering effort.

The project follows the implementation/demo format required by CEE 498 MLC. It reproduces the central idea of the physics-informed neural network paper by Raissi et al.: training a neural network using both a data-fitting loss and a physics/domain residual evaluated at collocation points. Because public queue data is tabular rather than a direct PDE simulation field, the reproduction is conceptual and methodological. The original PDE residual is replaced with a monotonicity residual based on the domain expectation that, holding grid context fixed, required upgrade cost should not decrease when requested MW capacity increases.

## 2. Related Work

Raissi, Perdikaris, and Karniadakis (2019) introduced physics-informed neural networks as a way to train neural networks while respecting known physical laws. In their setting, a neural network approximates an unknown field, and automatic differentiation is used to compute derivatives that appear in a governing PDE. Training minimizes a composite objective:

```text
L(theta) = L_data(theta) + lambda L_physics(theta)
```

The data term fits observations, while the physics term penalizes PDE residuals at collocation points. The paper demonstrates this idea on systems including Burgers' equation, Schrodinger equation, Allen-Cahn equation, Navier-Stokes flow, and Korteweg-de Vries dynamics. A recurring finding is that the physics term improves data efficiency and physical plausibility, especially when observations are sparse.

This project uses the same composite-loss structure but adapts the residual to interconnection queue modeling. Public queue records do not contain enough power-flow state information to solve the actual grid equations. However, queue economics are still constrained by grid physics and regulatory logic. Larger projects generally require at least as much transfer capability and can trigger larger upgrades than smaller projects at the same point of interconnection. The project therefore encodes a monotonicity prior in the cost classifier.

## 3. Dataset and Features

The intended real-data backbone is the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory Queued Up 2025 dataset, supplemented where possible by ISO/RTO queue exports from MISO, PJM, and CAISO. The proposal and progress report identify the following fields as central:

- Project identifier.
- ISO/RTO region.
- Fuel or resource type.
- Requested MW capacity.
- Filing year.
- Voltage level.
- Queue position.
- Point of interconnection or substation identifier.
- County or local siting area.
- Deliverability status and interconnection service type.
- Distance to nearby transmission.
- Renewable penetration proxy.
- Queue status.
- Study timeline.
- Network-upgrade cost estimate where available.

The codebase also includes a synthetic dataset generator so the submitted repository runs without bundling large or externally licensed public data files. The synthetic generator is not a replacement for the final public-data analysis; it is a reproducibility fallback that preserves the expected schema, class imbalance, missing cost labels, and known relationships described in the proposal and progress update.

The feature set used in the pipeline is:

- `log_mw_capacity`: log-transformed requested MW capacity.
- `iso_region`: categorical ISO/RTO identifier.
- `fuel_type`: categorical generation/storage resource type.
- `filing_year`: numeric filing year.
- `voltage_kv`: transmission voltage.
- `queue_position`: project position or proxy within the queue.
- `substation_congestion_24mo`: number of prior projects at the same point of interconnection within the prior two years.
- `county_queue_density`: number of projects in the same county-year cohort.
- `queue_cohort_size`: number of projects entering the same ISO/year cohort.
- `distance_to_transmission_miles`: site proximity proxy for transmission access.
- `renewable_penetration_pct`: regional renewable saturation proxy.
- `deliverability_status` and `interconnection_service`: requested interconnection products.

The cost-label subset is smaller than the full dataset, matching the progress report's finding that network upgrade cost is incomplete and concentrated among projects that reached later study phases.

## 4. Methodology

The pipeline implements interpretable baselines, nonlinear models, an uncertainty model, and the physics-informed ablation.

Withdrawal prediction is modeled as binary classification. Logistic regression with class weighting is used as the interpretable baseline, and a neural-network classifier is used to capture nonlinear interactions among ISO region, fuel type, MW capacity, filing year, and congestion.

Timeline prediction is modeled as regression. Ridge regression is used as the linear baseline, and a neural-network regressor is included for nonlinear effects. The main evaluation metrics are mean absolute error in months and R2.

Cost bucket prediction is modeled as four-class classification on rows with cost labels. The unconstrained model is a standard multilayer perceptron trained with cross-entropy. The physics-informed version uses the same architecture but adds a monotonic residual:

```text
L_total = L_cross_entropy + lambda L_monotonic
L_monotonic = mean(max(0, E[y | x] - E[y | x_high_mw]))
```

Here, `x_high_mw` is a collocation copy of the original project where requested MW is increased while all other features are held fixed. The expected bucket is computed from the softmax probabilities over ordered cost buckets. If the expected bucket decreases after increasing MW, the model receives a penalty.

Gaussian process regression is applied to log network-upgrade cost on the labeled subset. It reports predictive mean, standard deviation, and 90% prediction interval coverage. This addresses the proposal's goal of giving developers not only point estimates but also uncertainty estimates in data-sparse regions.

## 5. Experimental Setup

The pipeline creates an 80/10/10 train/validation/test split. Where feasible, the split is stratified by ISO region and filing-year cohort to reduce temporal and regional leakage. Preprocessing is fit only on the training data. Numeric variables are standardized and categorical variables are one-hot encoded.

The evaluation metrics follow the proposal:

- Withdrawal prediction: AUC-ROC, F1 for the withdrawn class, and accuracy.
- Cost bucket prediction: weighted F1 and accuracy.
- Timeline regression: MAE and R2.
- GPR uncertainty: prediction interval coverage probability at 90%.
- PINN ablation: cost accuracy/F1 and monotonic implausibility rate.

The current checked-in results were generated with:

```bash
PYTHONPATH=src python3 -m interconnection_ml.run_pipeline --output outputs
```

## 6. Results

The final report should pull exact sample sizes, label coverage, class balance, and model metrics from `outputs/metrics.json` after the final run. This avoids hand-entered values drifting out of sync with the executed code.

Withdrawal prediction should be discussed using AUC-ROC, withdrawn-class F1, and accuracy for both logistic regression and the MLP classifier. The key interpretation to check is whether congestion, local density, MW capacity, and filing-year pressure improve discrimination beyond the majority-class baseline.

Timeline prediction should be reported with MAE in months and R2 for both Ridge regression and the MLP regressor. If the linear model wins, that supports an interpretable additive story; if the MLP wins, that supports the proposal's nonlinear-interaction hypothesis.

Cost bucket classification should compare the unconstrained MLP against the monotonic PINN-style MLP using weighted F1, accuracy, and monotonic implausibility rate. The important ablation question is whether the physics-informed residual reduces implausible outputs without sacrificing predictive performance.

Gaussian process regression should be reported with log-cost MAE, log-cost R2, 90% prediction interval coverage, and mean interval width. The practical story is uncertainty: developers need to know when a cost estimate is weak because the model is extrapolating from sparse historical examples.

## 7. Discussion

The strongest project-level insight is that queue congestion and siting-context features are valuable. The substation congestion proxy, county queue density, ISO/year cohort size, distance to transmission, deliverability request, and renewable penetration proxy together tell a more complete story than MW capacity alone. These variables align with the project hypothesis that congested points of interconnection are more likely to produce costly upgrades, delays, and withdrawal.

A second insight is that label completeness strongly affects task difficulty. Withdrawal and timeline labels are nearly complete, so those models can be trained on the full dataset. Network-upgrade cost is available only for a subset, making the cost task more fragile and more dependent on missingness patterns.

The physics-informed model is most important as a modeling constraint. Even if the unconstrained model performs well on a clean synthetic dataset, real public queue data can be sparse and noisy. In that setting, the monotonic penalty should help prevent locally implausible predictions in parts of feature space with few observations, matching the motivation of Raissi et al.

## 8. Limitations and Future Work

The largest limitation is data quality. Public queue exports differ across ISO/RTOs, and substation names or project identifiers are not always standardized. The final public-data version should carefully document join logic and missingness.

A second limitation is that the monotonicity constraint is simplified. Actual upgrade costs depend on deliverability, thermal limits, voltage constraints, short-circuit duty, affected-system studies, and prior network upgrades. Requested MW is important, but not sufficient to determine cost. The monotonic penalty should be viewed as a useful regularizer rather than a complete power-flow model.

Future work should add richer grid-topology and transmission-capacity features, compare calibrated probabilistic classifiers for cost buckets, and revisit the deprioritized autoencoder for similar-project retrieval.
